CASCADE clean-air-in-schools calculator

Poorly ventilated classrooms raise the spread of respiratory infections and may hold back learning; the interventions that fix this cost money. This tool shows the conditions under which the social benefits exceed the costs.

CASCADE = Classroom Air, School-acquired Contagion, And Downstream Economics. It is a societal cost–benefit model, evaluated per classroom per year. A transparent companion to the decision model (Henderson et al., in preparation): the equations, every parameter, and the influence structure are shown and clickable.

Illustrative, not findings. Values are in New Zealand dollars (NZD), calibrated to realistic but approximate figures (sources shown when you click a parameter). The four keystone parameters (κ, σ, π, ρc) are not yet well estimated; treat outputs as a structured what-if, and verify before citing.

The model

$$\mathrm{NSB}_j=\delta\,n\,\bigl(B^{\mathrm{dir}}_j+B^{\mathrm{sec}}_j\bigr)-C_j$$ $$B^{\mathrm{dir}}_j=\underbrace{m\,\kappa\,\sigma_j\,\varphi\,\theta_j}_{\text{averted infections}}\bigl(p_{\mathrm{med}}c_{\mathrm{ch}}+u_{\mathrm{ch}}\lambda+\bar a\,\rho_{\mathrm{day}}\bigr)+\underbrace{g_j\,\rho_{\mathrm{SD}}}_{\text{cognitive}}$$ $$B^{\mathrm{sec}}_j=m\,\kappa\,\sigma_j\,\varphi\,\theta_j\bigl[c_{\mathrm{sup}}+M\,\psi_{w}\bigr]\qquad \theta_j=\frac{\eta_j\Delta q_j}{Q_0+\eta_j\Delta q_j}\qquad g_j=\beta\,\Delta\mathrm{CO}_2\,\pi\qquad M=\frac{\rho_c}{1-\rho_c}\qquad C_j=K\cdot\mathrm{CRF}(r,L)+E_{\mathrm{om}}$$

In this calculator the population-weighted secondary sum Σkψk(wkak+ck+ukλ) is collapsed into one weighted secondary-case cost ψw; adherence η is folded into the single delivered-clean-air slider (η·Δq); Eom is annual energy + maintenance.

How to read this

The headline is the net social benefit per classroom per year from a societal perspective: health-system savings + caregiver productivity + children's human capital, minus the annualised intervention cost (capital is spread over its service life via the capital-recovery factor). A positive figure means the intervention is worth it to society — not that it fits a school's operating budget. The cognitive channel values durable learning gains at lifetime-earnings figures, so it can dominate; that is genuine, but it leans on the persistence factor π, which is unmeasured.

Limitations — what the model does not capture

A short, deliberately honest list of what is left out, and which way each omission is likely to push the benefit-cost ratio (BCR).

Omissions that likely understate the BCR (true value higher)

  • Teacher and staff health. The model counts children and downstream household/community cases, but not averted infections, absence, or productivity loss among teachers and support staff who share the same air.
  • Non-respiratory and chronic effects. Cleaner air also reduces asthma exacerbations, allergy symptoms, and absence from sources other than acute respiratory infection; and CO₂/pollutant exposure may have effects beyond the modelled test-score channel.
  • Equity weighting. Benefits are unweighted. Disadvantaged children often have the highest baseline burden and the worst-ventilated buildings, so an equity-weighted social evaluation would typically raise the return on prioritising them.
  • Option and resilience value. Installed clean-air capacity has value in a future pandemic or wildfire-smoke event that a steady-state endemic model ignores.
  • Co-benefits of ventilation. Thermal comfort, odour, and general wellbeing/alertness effects are not valued.

Omissions that may overstate the BCR (true value lower)

  • Cognitive persistence (π) and fade-out. Valuing a one-year test-score gain at full lifetime earnings assumes durability that is not established; if gains fade, the dominant cognitive term shrinks sharply. This is the single biggest source of optimism.
  • Attribution upper bounds. κ comes from school-closure evidence (an upper bound on school-acquired infection) and σ is unestimated; if the treated rooms address less transmission than assumed, infection benefits fall.
  • Adherence and real-world operation. Devices switched off, windows closed, filters unmaintained, or lamps degraded all reduce delivered clean air below the rated figure entered here.
  • Behavioural offsetting. Perceived protection could change mixing or hygiene behaviour, partially offsetting gains (not modelled).
  • Double-counting risks at the margins. The downstream multiplier M is bounded to exclude school-acquired and within-room cases, but in practice these boundaries are hard to measure cleanly.

Structural simplifications (direction ambiguous)

  • Single representative classroom, endemic steady state. No dynamic transmission, no network/general-equilibrium effects, no economies or diseconomies of scale across a whole school or system.
  • Concave (Wells–Riley-style) risk reduction. θ is exact only in the linear regime; for high per-exposure risk the true reduction is smaller.
  • Collapsed secondary mix. The recipient-type sum is reduced to one weighted secondary-case cost ψw; the real mix of working-age adults, siblings and older adults carries different productivity and mortality profiles.
  • Jurisdiction transfer. Dollar values are approximate NZD; human-capital and QALY figures are especially sensitive to the chosen jurisdiction.
Using these results responsibly. This is a transparent what-if built on illustrative parameters, several of them unmeasured. Use it to explore which assumptions matter and to frame an evidence agenda — not to quote a specific benefit-cost ratio as an established estimate. The headline figure for cheap, CO₂-lowering measures is dominated by the uncertain cognitive channel and should be read as “potentially large but unproven”.
